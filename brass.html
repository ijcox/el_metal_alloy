Brass
Brass is a copper-zinc alloy known for its corrosion resistance, attractive gold-like appearance, and excellent machinability.

Brass is one of the most widely used copper alloys. It is primarily composed of copper and zinc, with the zinc content determining its strength, color, and mechanical properties. Additional elements such as lead, tin, aluminum, or manganese may be added to enhance specific characteristics.

Brass is valued for its workability, low friction, conductivity, and decorative appeal. It is commonly used in plumbing fittings, musical instruments, hardware, and architectural applications.

Composition

Element Typical Percentage Purpose
Copper (Cu) 55–95% Base metal providing corrosion resistance and conductivity
Zinc (Zn) 5–45% Increases strength and hardness
Other Elements Varies Improve machinability, strength, or corrosion resistance

Key Properties

  • Excellent corrosion resistance
  • Good electrical and thermal conductivity
  • High machinability
  • Low friction properties
  • Non-magnetic
  • Attractive gold-like color

Types of Brass

Cartridge Brass

Contains about 70% copper and 30% zinc. Highly ductile and used in ammunition casings.

Naval Brass

Contains small amounts of tin for improved corrosion resistance in marine environments.

Free-Cutting Brass

Includes lead for enhanced machinability in precision parts.

Red Brass

Higher copper content, offering superior corrosion resistance and strength.

Applications

  • Plumbing fittings and valves
  • Musical instruments (trumpets, trombones, saxophones)
  • Decorative hardware and fixtures
  • Electrical connectors and terminals
  • Bearings and gears
  • Coins and medals
  • Radiators and heat exchangers

Manufacturing Process

Brass is typically produced by melting copper and adding zinc in controlled proportions. The molten alloy is then cast into shapes or processed through extrusion, rolling, forging, or machining.

Advantages

  • Highly workable and easy to machine
  • Corrosion resistant
  • Visually appealing
  • Durable and long-lasting

Disadvantages

  • Less strong than steel
  • Can dezincify in certain corrosive environments
  • More expensive than some base metals

Maintenance and Care

  • Clean with mild soap and water
  • Polish to maintain shine
  • Allow natural patina for antique look
  • Avoid harsh chemicals that cause tarnish
Brass combines durability, corrosion resistance, and aesthetic appeal, making it one of the most versatile and widely used metal alloys in both functional and decorative applications.
