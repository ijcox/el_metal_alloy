Metal Alloys
Metal alloys are materials created by combining two or more elements, at least one of which is a metal, to improve strength, durability, corrosion resistance, or other properties.

A metal alloy is formed when metals are mixed together or combined with other elements such as carbon, silicon, or nickel. The purpose is usually to improve mechanical strength, corrosion resistance, hardness, weight, conductivity, or temperature tolerance compared to pure metals.

Alloys are essential to modern engineering and manufacturing and are used in everything from construction and electronics to aircraft, medical devices, and tools.

For detailed information about individual alloys, visit pages such as Bronze, Brass, Steel, and Stainless Steel.

Why Alloys Are Used

Reason Description
Strength Many alloys are significantly stronger than pure metals.
Corrosion Resistance Some alloys resist rust and chemical damage.
Hardness Alloys can be engineered to resist wear and deformation.
Temperature Resistance Special alloys maintain strength at extremely high or low temperatures.
Weight Reduction Lightweight alloys such as aluminum alloys are used in aircraft and vehicles.
Electrical Performance Alloys may improve conductivity or resistance depending on application.

How Metal Alloys Are Made

Alloys are typically created by melting the base metals together in a furnace and allowing them to mix uniformly. Once combined, the molten metal is cooled and solidified into ingots, sheets, or other shapes.

Common manufacturing processes include:

  • Melting and casting
  • Powder metallurgy
  • Mechanical alloying
  • Electrochemical alloying
  • Heat treatment and tempering

Properties of Metal Alloys

Property Description
Hardness Resistance to scratching or deformation.
Tensile Strength Maximum stress an alloy can withstand before breaking.
Ductility Ability to stretch without breaking.
Conductivity Ability to conduct heat or electricity.
Corrosion Resistance Resistance to oxidation, rust, and chemical damage.
Melting Point The temperature at which the alloy becomes liquid.
